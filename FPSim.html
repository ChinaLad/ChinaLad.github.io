Fluid Particle Simulation

Pressure

Particles in fluid like to stay together while also having some distance between each other. That means there must be some attraction force between the particles, but there must also be a repulsion force so the particles have some distance between them.

That means initially, the attraction force is larger and as the particles get closer, the repulsion force increases faster than the attraction force and at some point becomes larger.

Viscosity

Viscosity in a real fluid represents its resistance to deformation or flow. It determines how "thick" or "sticky" the fluid is. Viscosity can make a big difference: think of the difference between honey and water.

In the simulation, viscosity is modeled by adjusting particle velocities based on their relative motion, which helps replicate the internal friction present in real fluids. This ensures that the simulated fluid exhibits realistic flow behaviors, such as slowing down as it moves or resisting rapid deformation.

Elasticity and Plasticity

Elasticity refers to a fluid's ability to return to its original shape after deformation, while plasticity is the fluid's ability to undergo permanent deformation. Some fluids may retain some shape (like mud).

In the simulation, these properties are represented by spring-like forces between particles that adjust based on their positions. There is spring displacement and adjustment of the springs.

The spring displacement is responsible for the elastic properties of the fluid. It displaces the particles based on the resting lenght of the spring, the current lenght, and the srping coefficient.

The spring adjustment is responsible for the plastic properties of the fluid. When the spring length exceeds a certain threshold, the resting length of the string is adjusted a little bit, so part of the deformation is retained.

Optimization

Naturally, a water molecule doesn't directly affect another water molecule that is 1km away from it. That is why all the above formulas use a constant that represents a radius of effect. If the distance between two particles exceedss this radius, then the forces between these particles will be 0.

There is no force between distant particles, but the computations will still be done for the pair. With just a few particles in the simulation, the number of computations can get massive. That is why by dividing the simulation space into a grid can reduce the number of computations. For each particle we find its cell and the cells neighbouring it and we make calculationw with the particles in these 9 cells.
